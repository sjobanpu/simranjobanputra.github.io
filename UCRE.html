RIDESHARE+

Timeline: One Semester (Spring 2018)

My Role: Interview Lead, Strategy

Target User: Anyone who rides the public bus - students (grad or undergrad), faculty

Key Methods: Affinity Diagramming, Survey Design, Hueristic Evaluation, Interviewing, Storyboarding, Stakeholder Mapping



RIDESHARE+ is a pool feature that Carnegie Mellon students can use as safe and inexpensive alternative to the Port Authority Bus System.

In September 2018, budgets were cut for Port Authority in order to fund a suffering Pennsylvania state budget. In turn, this led to massive issues
for the population of Pittsburgh, which is heavily reliant on public transportation due to its large student population (University of Pittsburgh,
Carnegie Mellon University, Point Park University, Duquesne University, and Chatham to name a few). RIDESHARE+ could be used by students to
find a cheap and safer alternative to and from campus.

We conducted our case study with a focus on Carnegie Mellon students. Once a student is registered, they can download the Uber app and get a
code that can activate the CMU Uber extension. This gives students two new options: a CMU Pool, which provides cheaper rides to a regular Uber
during late night study sessions on campus, or CMU Uber, which gives CMU students preference and helps students get to their location in a much
faster time that is also fairly inexpensive. This venture can be funded by the pool of money that is already allocated to transportation from student
tuition, which makes it easy for Carnegie Mellon - they no longer have to spend extra money on a temporary solution. And, by targeting a niche
space, Uber will gain more revenue and build better relationships with students for future rides.

My team and I conducting affinity diagramming to better understand our stakeholders' daily routines and problems.

Funding & Competitive Analysis

RIDESHARE+ was a better option in comparison to current options provided by Carnegie Mellon for safety and convenience.

Currently, Carnegie Mellon has money that is specifically allocated towards transportation. In the implementation of Rideshare, that money would be
used to pay towards Uber rides instead of for each tap on the bus system, saving Carnegie Mellon the need to pay for temporary workers and extra buses.





Future Extensions

As a chair on student cabinet, I was actually able to push for negotiations with Uber to bring Uber pool to campus.

Many universities around the US now have relationships with Uber and Lyft to have late night ride programs. For example, USC has this contract with Lyft.
Since Carnegie Mellon already has a strong relationship with Uber and a campus in Pittsburgh, we got in touch with Uber executives to help them
understand why introducing Uber Pool to the campus area would be a great idea. The negotiations are still in the works. We had significant roadblocks
due to the size of the Pittsburgh population itself, however, I think the number of temporary residents (i.e. students) isn't taken into account in this space.
This was a great experience for me - applying my work from the classroom into the real world and I hope the negotiations are successful in the future.

In terms of the future of this program, Carnegie Mellon could also begin to use this service to encourage students to explore Pittsburgh more often, since it
is much more convenient to get downtown. This would allow Uber to get longer rides and increased revenue from students and in turn, could be a really
great opportunity for Carnegie Mellon to shed light on the Pittsburgh community.
